The Business Challenge

Bringing the record shop counter online

Round & Round Records (R&R) is a small independent retailer of vinyl records and CDs. Their differentiator has always been personal, knowledgeable service: the staff act as music guides, helping customers find hard-to-get records, advising on different pressings and versions, and introducing people to new music based on their existing tastes.

The problem: business has increasingly moved online, where customers browse and search for items on a screen. There's no opportunity for the friendly, expert advice that makes R&R special. How do you replicate that personal touch digitally?

The answer: a friendly, AI-powered chatbot named Bob that mimics the experience of talking to a knowledgeable staff member in the physical shop.

How Bob Works

AI-powered music discovery, grounded in real catalogues

🤖

Conversational AI

Bob is powered by DeepSeek, a large language model. He understands natural conversation: you can say "I like Pixies and Metric" or "best Irish albums of the 90s" and he'll have a genuinely knowledgeable chat about music, just like a real record shop assistant.

💿

Real Catalogue Data

Bob uses Last.fm's massive music database to find similar artists, top albums by play count, genre tags, artist biographies, and even upcoming live events. Everything he suggests is grounded in real data.

🛒

Purchase Path

Every album Bob suggests links to Discogs, the world's largest physical music marketplace. Since R&R Records is a fictional business created for this assignment and has no real catalogue, Discogs serves as a proxy for what would be the shop's actual inventory in a real deployment.

Limitations & Known Weak Spots

Where Bob still struggles

Like any real-world AI project, Bob has rough edges. Here are the honest ones:

🔎

NLP Entity Extraction

Bob uses a second LLM call to extract artist and album names from his own responses for clickable links. This is far more accurate than regex, but it adds latency. The regex fallback (used when the LLM call is slow) is brittle: it strips common conversational words but can still extract phrases like "the early stuff" as artist names. Multi-word artist names with common words (e.g. "The The", "!!!") are particularly challenging.

💿

Discogs Data Quality

The Discogs catalogue is community-maintained and messy. The same release often exists under multiple entries with different titles: "OK Computer", "OK Computer (1997 Release)", "OK Computer - Radiohead". Our fuzzy matching handles most cases, but some albums simply won't be found because the Last.fm album name doesn't match any Discogs title variant. The "Buy Now" detection also relies on a single release match, so if the top result is a bootleg or wrong pressing, the link may be suboptimal.

⚠

Conversation Context

Bob maintains conversation history (last 10 exchanges) within the current session, and persists memory across visits using browser localStorage — including your name, recent searches, and favourite genres. On return visits, Bob greets you personally and references past chats. This memory is device-only: no data leaves your browser. A "Clear memory" button lets you wipe it at any time.

⏰

API Latency

A single user query triggers up to 4 parallel API calls (Last.fm similar artists, Last.fm top albums, Last.fm tags, and the main LLM response). The entity extraction adds a 5th LLM call. On slow connections, the discovery panel can take 2-4 seconds to populate. The LLM response appears first (STEP 1), but the album grid and purchase badges fill in later (STEP 2), creating a two-phase load experience.

💾

Browser Memory

Bob uses localStorage to remember your preferences, recent searches, and your name between visits — no login required. Everything stays on your device; nothing is sent to a server. Memory is per-browser (not synced across devices). A "Clear memory" button in the chat header wipes all stored data instantly.

🎧

Genre & Discovery Accuracy

Genre-based searches ("best Irish albums of the 80s") use Last.fm's community-applied tags, which are crowd-sourced and can be inconsistent. An album tagged "irish" might be Irish folk, Irish rock, or just by an artist who once played in Dublin. There's no era filtering at the API level, so "80s Irish albums" returns all Irish-tagged albums regardless of decade. Bob's LLM compensates with its own knowledge, but the panel data is unfiltered.

Technical Architecture

Secure, serverless, and key-safe

All three APIs (DeepSeek, Last.fm, Discogs) require secret keys. These are never exposed to the browser. A Cloudflare Worker acts as a thin proxy layer: the browser calls the worker, the worker injects the secret keys server-side, and the response flows back. No keys in source code, no keys in DevTools.

Development Approach

Built by a team of AI agent colleagues

The project was developed using a structured team of specialist AI agents, each with a defined role, domain expertise, and boundaries. This mirrors how a real innovation team would operate.

The team's design decisions are grounded in three academic best-practice frameworks from the Customer Engagement & AI module: the seven-dimension chatbot taxonomy (Adamopoulou & Moussiades 2020), CASA / Computers Are Social Actors (Reeves & Nass 1996), and conversation flow anatomy. Each specialist agent owns the framework dimensions relevant to their domain — from @Fionn (domain closure) through @Siobhan (CASA disclosure in UI), @Dot (recovery ladder implementation), @Niamh (localStorage consent), and @Keelin (cross-framework QA appraisal).
